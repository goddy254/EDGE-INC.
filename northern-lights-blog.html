Capturing the Northern Lights: A Photographer's Journey in Iceland

By Godfrey Kamau Published on March 15, 2023 Category: Landscape Photography 8 min read
The Aurora Borealis dances over Þingvellir National Park, Iceland

There are few natural phenomena as breathtaking as the Northern Lights. As a landscape photographer, capturing the aurora borealis had been on my bucket list for years. Last winter, I finally made the journey to Iceland, one of the best places on Earth to witness and photograph this celestial spectacle.

Preparing for the Shoot

Photographing the Northern Lights requires careful preparation. After extensive research, I packed:

  • Full-frame camera with excellent low-light performance (I used the Sony A7R IV)
  • Fast wide-angle lens (Sony 14mm f/1.8 GM)
  • Sturdy tripod
  • Extra batteries (cold drains them quickly)
  • Hand warmers (for both me and my equipment)
  • Remote shutter release

Camera Settings for Aurora Photography

After several nights of trial and error, I found these settings worked best for my setup:

My typical Northern Lights camera settings
Setting Value Notes
Aperture f/1.8 As wide as your lens allows
Shutter Speed 8-15 seconds Adjust based on aurora movement
ISO 1600-3200 Higher for faint auroras
White Balance 3500-4000K Cooler temperatures work well
Focus Manual Focus on stars or distant lights

The Magic of the First Night

My first successful capture came on a freezing night near Kirkjufell mountain. The KP index (measuring aurora activity) was at 5, promising a strong display. As I set up my gear, faint green streaks began to appear in the northern sky.

Within minutes, the entire sky erupted in swirling greens and purples. The aurora moved so quickly that I had to adjust my shutter speed to 5 seconds to avoid blurring the intricate patterns. That night yielded some of my favorite images from the trip.

Kirkjufell mountain under the Northern Lights

Post-Processing Tips

Raw files from aurora photography often need careful processing to bring out the best in the images:

  1. Adjust white balance to maintain natural colors
  2. Boost shadows carefully to reveal foreground detail
  3. Enhance contrast to make the aurora stand out
  4. Reduce noise while preserving detail
  5. Consider selective color adjustments

Final Thoughts

Photographing the Northern Lights was both challenging and immensely rewarding. Beyond the technical aspects, the experience of standing under the dancing lights in the Icelandic wilderness was truly magical. For any photographer considering this adventure, my advice is simple: be prepared, be patient, and don't forget to put the camera down occasionally to simply enjoy the show.

"The Northern Lights don't just appear in your viewfinder—they dance across your soul, leaving traces of magic no sensor can capture."
